## Why Logistic Regression Reporting Is Challenging

Logistic regression is one of the most widely used statistical techniques in health sciences, psychology, and education research. Unlike a t-test or ANOVA, where you report a single test statistic and an effect size, logistic regression produces a complex array of output that must be organized into a coherent narrative.

A complete logistic regression write-up involves overall model fit statistics, pseudo R-squared values, classification accuracy, and individual predictor statistics including *B* coefficients, standard errors, Wald statistics, *p* values, odds ratios, and confidence intervals. Missing any one of these elements is a common reason for revision requests from journal reviewers.

This guide walks through every component step by step, with concrete APA 7th edition examples you can adapt for your own manuscript.

## The Key Statistics to Report

Before diving into the reporting templates, here is a summary of the statistics that belong in a logistic regression write-up.

**Overall model fit:**
- Omnibus chi-square test (model chi-square)
- Degrees of freedom and *p* value
- **Pseudo R-squared** values: Nagelkerke *R*² and Cox & Snell *R*²

**Classification performance:**
- Overall classification accuracy (percentage correct)
- Sensitivity (true positive rate) and specificity (true negative rate)

**Individual predictors:**
- *B* (unstandardized logistic regression coefficient)
- *SE* (standard error of *B*)
- **Wald** chi-square statistic
- *p* value
- **OR** (odds ratio, also labeled Exp(*B*) in SPSS output)
- 95% CI for the odds ratio

Each of these plays a distinct role. The model fit statistics tell readers whether the set of predictors collectively distinguishes between the two outcome groups. The classification table shows how well the model performs in practice. The individual predictor statistics reveal which variables drive the prediction and by how much.

## Step 1: Report Overall Model Fit

The omnibus test of model coefficients evaluates whether the full model with predictors fits significantly better than a null model with only the intercept. This is reported as a chi-square test.

**APA template:**

> A binary logistic regression was performed to examine the effects of [predictors] on [outcome]. The overall model was statistically significant, *chi-square*(*df*) = X.XX, *p* = .XXX, indicating that the predictors, as a set, reliably distinguished between [group 1] and [group 2]. The model explained XX.X% (Nagelkerke *R*²) of the variance in [outcome].

**Example:**

> A binary logistic regression was performed to examine the effects of GPA, weekly study hours, and class attendance on graduation status. The overall model was statistically significant, *chi-square*(3) = 34.72, *p* < .001, indicating that the predictors, as a set, reliably distinguished between students who graduated and those who did not. The model explained 31.5% (Nagelkerke *R*²) of the variance in graduation status.

### Understanding Pseudo R-Squared

Unlike ordinary least squares regression, logistic regression does not produce a true *R*² value. Instead, it offers **pseudo R-squared** measures that approximate the proportion of variance explained.

| Measure | Range | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| Cox & Snell *R*² | 0 to < 1 | Cannot reach 1.0; tends to underestimate |
| Nagelkerke *R*² | 0 to 1 | Adjusted version of Cox & Snell; preferred for reporting |

Most APA-style articles report **Nagelkerke *R*²** because its upper bound is 1.0, making it more interpretable. Some researchers report both values for transparency. Either approach is acceptable, but always label which pseudo R-squared you are using.

## Step 2: Report the Classification Table

The classification table summarizes how accurately the model assigns cases to the correct outcome group. This is a practical measure of model performance that complements the statistical significance tests.

**APA template:**

> The model correctly classified XX.X% of cases overall, with a sensitivity of XX.X% (correctly predicted [positive outcome]) and a specificity of XX.X% (correctly predicted [negative outcome]).

**Example:**

> The model correctly classified 78.3% of cases overall, with a sensitivity of 82.1% (correctly predicted graduation) and a specificity of 71.4% (correctly predicted non-graduation).

### Classification Table Format

| Observed | Predicted: Did Not Graduate | Predicted: Graduated | Percentage Correct |
|----------|----------------------------|---------------------|--------------------|
| Did Not Graduate | 45 | 18 | 71.4% |
| Graduated | 15 | 69 | 82.1% |
| Overall | | | 78.3% |

When interpreting classification accuracy, consider the **base rate**. If 70% of students in your sample graduated, a model that simply predicts everyone graduates would achieve 70% accuracy with no predictors. Your model's accuracy should be evaluated against this baseline.

## Step 3: Report Individual Predictors

Individual predictor results are best presented in a table followed by a narrative description. The APA-formatted table for logistic regression includes columns for *B*, *SE*, Wald chi-square, *p*, odds ratio (OR), and the 95% confidence interval for the odds ratio.

### Logistic Regression Coefficients Table

| Predictor | *B* | *SE* | Wald *chi-square* | *p* | OR | 95% CI for OR |
|-----------|------|------|-----------|------|------|---------------|
| (Constant) | -8.42 | 2.15 | 15.33 | < .001 | -- | -- |
| GPA | 1.63 | 0.52 | 9.82 | .002 | 5.10 | [1.84, 14.15] |
| Study hours | 0.18 | 0.07 | 6.61 | .010 | 1.20 | [1.04, 1.37] |
| Attendance (%) | 0.04 | 0.02 | 4.00 | .045 | 1.04 | [1.00, 1.08] |

### Writing Up the Individual Predictors

> GPA was a significant predictor of graduation status, *B* = 1.63, *SE* = 0.52, Wald *chi-square*(1) = 9.82, *p* = .002, OR = 5.10, 95% CI [1.84, 14.15]. For each one-point increase in GPA, the odds of graduating were approximately 5.10 times greater. Weekly study hours also significantly predicted graduation, *B* = 0.18, *SE* = 0.07, Wald *chi-square*(1) = 6.61, *p* = .010, OR = 1.20, 95% CI [1.04, 1.37]. Each additional hour of weekly study was associated with a 20% increase in the odds of graduating. Class attendance percentage was a marginally significant predictor, *B* = 0.04, *SE* = 0.02, Wald *chi-square*(1) = 4.00, *p* = .045, OR = 1.04, 95% CI [1.00, 1.08].

Note that the Wald chi-square has 1 degree of freedom for each individual predictor (assuming the predictor is not a multi-category variable with dummy coding). Always include the degrees of freedom in parentheses.

## Interpreting Odds Ratios

The **odds ratio** (OR) is the primary effect size measure in logistic regression. It tells you how the odds of the outcome change with a one-unit increase in the predictor, holding all other predictors constant.

### Odds Ratio Reference Guide

| OR Value | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| OR = 1.00 | No effect; predictor does not change the odds |
| OR > 1.00 | Increased odds of the outcome |
| OR < 1.00 | Decreased odds of the outcome |

**Concrete example:** An OR of 2.45 means that for a one-unit increase in the predictor, the odds of the outcome occurring are **2.45 times greater** (or 145% higher). An OR of 0.60 means that for a one-unit increase, the odds **decrease by 40%** (calculated as 1 - 0.60 = 0.40).

### Continuous vs. Categorical Predictors

For **continuous predictors**, the OR reflects a one-unit change on the original measurement scale. If study hours is measured in hours per week, OR = 1.20 means each additional hour increases the odds by 20%. Be mindful of the unit: if the variable were measured in minutes, the OR would be much smaller per unit and harder to interpret. Consider rescaling continuous predictors (e.g., per 10-hour increase) if the per-unit OR is very close to 1.00.

For **categorical predictors** (dummy coded), the OR compares the odds of the outcome in the coded group to the reference group. If treatment group (coded 1) versus control group (coded 0) has OR = 3.20, the treatment group has 3.20 times the odds of the outcome compared to the control group.

### Important Distinction: Odds Are Not Probabilities

A common error is interpreting an odds ratio as a probability ratio. Saying "patients in the treatment group were 3.20 times more likely to recover" is technically imprecise. The correct phrasing is "the odds of recovery were 3.20 times greater in the treatment group." When the outcome is rare (less than 10% prevalence), the odds ratio approximates the risk ratio, but for common outcomes, the two diverge substantially.

## Complete APA Reporting Example

Below is a full write-up combining all elements from the previous sections. This example uses the graduation prediction scenario with three predictors.

> A binary logistic regression was conducted to predict graduation status (graduated vs. did not graduate) from GPA, weekly study hours, and class attendance percentage among 147 undergraduate students. The overall model was statistically significant, *chi-square*(3) = 34.72, *p* < .001, Nagelkerke *R*² = .32, indicating that the set of predictors reliably distinguished between students who graduated and those who did not. The model correctly classified 78.3% of cases, with a sensitivity of 82.1% and a specificity of 71.4%.
>
> GPA was the strongest predictor of graduation, *B* = 1.63, *SE* = 0.52, Wald *chi-square*(1) = 9.82, *p* = .002, OR = 5.10, 95% CI [1.84, 14.15]. For each one-point increase in GPA, the odds of graduating were approximately five times greater. Weekly study hours also significantly predicted graduation, *B* = 0.18, *SE* = 0.07, Wald *chi-square*(1) = 6.61, *p* = .010, OR = 1.20, 95% CI [1.04, 1.37], with each additional hour associated with a 20% increase in odds. Class attendance made a smaller but statistically significant contribution, *B* = 0.04, *SE* = 0.02, Wald *chi-square*(1) = 4.00, *p* = .045, OR = 1.04, 95% CI [1.00, 1.08].

This example follows a clear structure: state the analysis and sample, report overall model fit and classification accuracy, then describe each predictor with its full set of statistics. Reviewers can quickly locate every required piece of information.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Reporting B Coefficients Without Odds Ratios

The *B* coefficient in logistic regression is a log-odds value, which is not intuitively interpretable. Always convert *B* to the odds ratio (OR = e^*B*) and report both. The odds ratio is the effect size that readers and reviewers expect to see.

### Omitting Confidence Intervals for Odds Ratios

The 95% confidence interval for the OR is essential. It communicates the precision of the odds ratio estimate and reveals whether the effect could be trivially small or substantially large. An OR of 2.50 with a 95% CI of [0.85, 7.35] crosses 1.00 and would not be statistically significant, whereas an OR of 2.50 with a CI of [1.40, 4.46] provides much stronger evidence.

### Confusing Odds Ratios With Probabilities

As noted above, odds and probabilities are mathematically different. An OR of 3.00 does not mean the outcome is "three times as likely." It means the odds are three times greater. This distinction matters most when the outcome prevalence is high.

### Not Reporting Model Fit Statistics

Some researchers jump directly to individual predictors without reporting the omnibus model test, Nagelkerke *R*², or classification accuracy. Without these, readers cannot evaluate whether the overall model is meaningful before examining individual effects.

### Using R-Squared Instead of Pseudo R-Squared

Logistic regression does not produce a traditional *R*² value. Reporting *R*² = .32 without specifying that it is Nagelkerke *R*² (or Cox & Snell *R*²) is misleading. Always label the type of pseudo R-squared you are reporting.

### Ignoring the Hosmer-Lemeshow Test

The **Hosmer-Lemeshow goodness-of-fit test** evaluates whether the model fits the data well. A non-significant result (*p* > .05) indicates adequate fit. While not always required, reporting this test strengthens your write-up, especially when reviewers are concerned about model calibration.

## Reporting Odds Ratios With Confidence Intervals

The odds ratio alone communicates only a point estimate. Without a confidence interval, readers cannot assess the precision of the effect or determine whether the true population odds ratio might be trivially small or dramatically large. APA 7th edition explicitly requires confidence intervals for effect size measures, and in logistic regression, the 95% CI for each odds ratio serves this role.

### APA Format for Odds Ratios With Confidence Intervals

The standard format integrates the OR and its CI into a single clause within the results narrative:

> Predictor *X* was a significant predictor of the outcome, OR = 3.42, 95% CI [1.87, 6.25], *p* < .001.

When presenting odds ratios in a table, the confidence interval is placed in its own column using square brackets:

| Predictor | OR | 95% CI |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Age | 1.08 | [1.03, 1.14] |
| Gender (female) | 2.31 | [1.15, 4.63] |
| Education (college+) | 0.54 | [0.28, 1.04] |

### Interpreting the Confidence Interval

The 95% CI tells you the range within which the true population odds ratio is likely to fall with 95% confidence. Two key interpretation rules apply:

**Rule 1: Does the interval cross 1.00?** If the lower bound is below 1.00 and the upper bound is above 1.00, the odds ratio is not statistically significant at the .05 level. An OR of 1.80 with a CI of [0.72, 4.50] means the effect could range from a 28% decrease to a 350% increase in odds, providing no clear evidence of a directional effect.

**Rule 2: How wide is the interval?** A narrow CI (e.g., [2.10, 2.90]) indicates a precise estimate, whereas a wide CI (e.g., [0.50, 12.40]) suggests considerable uncertainty. Wide intervals typically result from small sample sizes or low event rates.

### Forest Plots for Visualizing Odds Ratios

A **forest plot** is the standard visual display for odds ratios and their confidence intervals. Each predictor is shown as a row with a point estimate (the OR) and a horizontal line (the CI). A vertical reference line at OR = 1.00 marks the null hypothesis of no effect.

Forest plots are particularly valuable when reporting models with many predictors because they allow readers to immediately see which predictors have confidence intervals that exclude 1.00 (significant effects) and which have intervals that cross the reference line (non-significant effects).

In APA-style manuscripts, forest plots are presented as figures with a caption that describes the model and outcome variable. Label the x-axis as "Odds Ratio (95% CI)" and use a logarithmic scale when odds ratios span a wide range.

### Adjusted vs. Unadjusted Odds Ratios

An **unadjusted** (crude) odds ratio comes from a bivariate analysis with a single predictor. An **adjusted** odds ratio comes from a multivariable model that controls for other covariates. In most research contexts, you should report adjusted odds ratios because they account for confounding.

When your study involves both unadjusted and adjusted analyses, present them in parallel columns:

| Predictor | Unadjusted OR [95% CI] | Adjusted OR [95% CI] |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Smoking | 3.12 [1.85, 5.26] | 2.74 [1.52, 4.94] |
| BMI (per unit) | 1.09 [1.04, 1.15] | 1.06 [1.00, 1.12] |
| Exercise (weekly) | 0.68 [0.49, 0.94] | 0.75 [0.52, 1.08] |

This comparison reveals how confounding variables influence each predictor's relationship with the outcome.

## Model Fit Statistics in Detail

Reporting individual predictor results without establishing that the overall model fits the data is a common omission that weakens a logistic regression write-up. Model fit statistics answer a fundamental question: does this set of predictors, collectively, do a meaningfully better job of predicting the outcome than chance alone?

### Nagelkerke R-Squared

Nagelkerke *R*² is the most commonly reported pseudo R-squared in APA-style logistic regression. It adjusts the Cox & Snell *R*² so that its maximum possible value is 1.0, making it more interpretable. However, its absolute values are typically lower than the *R*² values seen in ordinary linear regression.

**Interpreting Nagelkerke R-squared:**

| Nagelkerke *R*² | Rough Interpretation |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| .02 to .12 | Small effect |
| .13 to .25 | Medium effect |
| .26 and above | Large effect |

These benchmarks are approximate and domain-dependent. A Nagelkerke *R*² of .15 may be impressive in a field where prediction is inherently difficult (e.g., predicting rare diseases from demographic variables) but modest in a field where high accuracy is expected (e.g., predicting diagnosis from clinical biomarkers).

**APA reporting format:**

> The model explained 28.4% of the variance in treatment response (Nagelkerke *R*² = .284).

### Hosmer-Lemeshow Goodness-of-Fit Test

The **Hosmer-Lemeshow test** evaluates how well the predicted probabilities match the observed outcomes across subgroups of the data. The test divides cases into decile groups based on predicted probabilities and compares observed and expected frequencies within each group using a chi-square statistic.

A **non-significant** result (*p* > .05) indicates adequate model fit, meaning the model's predictions align reasonably well with observed outcomes. A **significant** result (*p* < .05) suggests that the model does not fit the data well and that predicted probabilities deviate substantially from observed proportions.

**APA reporting format:**

> The Hosmer-Lemeshow test indicated adequate model fit, *chi-square*(8) = 6.42, *p* = .600.

**Important caveats:** The Hosmer-Lemeshow test is sensitive to sample size. With very large samples (N > 500), the test may flag trivial deviations as significant, leading to rejection of an otherwise adequate model. With very small samples, the test has low power and may fail to detect genuine lack of fit. Some methodologists recommend supplementing it with calibration plots.

### Classification Accuracy and the Confusion Matrix

Classification accuracy is the most intuitive model fit measure: what percentage of cases does the model classify correctly? However, it must be interpreted in context.

**Complete classification metrics to report:**

| Metric | Formula | Interpretation |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Overall accuracy | (TP + TN) / N | Percentage of all cases correctly classified |
| Sensitivity | TP / (TP + FN) | Correctly predicted positive outcomes |
| Specificity | TN / (TN + FP) | Correctly predicted negative outcomes |
| Positive predictive value | TP / (TP + FP) | Among predicted positives, how many are true |
| Negative predictive value | TN / (TN + FN) | Among predicted negatives, how many are true |

Where TP = true positives, TN = true negatives, FP = false positives, FN = false negatives.

**APA reporting format for classification:**

> The model correctly classified 81.2% of cases (sensitivity = 76.5%, specificity = 85.3%). The positive predictive value was 79.6%, and the negative predictive value was 83.1%. This represents a 15.2 percentage-point improvement over the base rate classification of 66.0%.

Always compare your model's accuracy to the **base rate** (the percentage of the majority class). A model that achieves 80% accuracy is uninformative if 80% of cases belong to one category, because simply predicting the majority class for every case would achieve the same accuracy with no predictors.

### AUC-ROC: The Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve

The **AUC** (Area Under the Curve) from a **ROC** (Receiver Operating Characteristic) analysis provides a threshold-independent measure of model discrimination. Unlike classification accuracy, which depends on a single cutoff (usually .50), the AUC evaluates the model's ability to distinguish between outcomes across all possible cutoff values.

| AUC Value | Discrimination |
|-----------|---------------|
| .50 | No discrimination (chance level) |
| .60 - .69 | Poor |
| .70 - .79 | Acceptable |
| .80 - .89 | Excellent |
| .90+ | Outstanding |

**APA reporting format:**

> The area under the ROC curve was .84, 95% CI [.78, .90], indicating excellent discrimination between treatment responders and non-responders.

## Multiple Predictors in Logistic Regression

Most applied logistic regression models include multiple predictors simultaneously. Reporting these results clearly requires careful organization to prevent readers from losing track of the individual contributions.

### Reporting Multiple Odds Ratios

When reporting three or more predictors, use a table as the primary presentation format rather than embedding every statistic in the text. The narrative should then highlight the most important findings without repeating every number from the table.

**APA table format for multiple predictors:**

| Predictor | *B* | *SE* | Wald | *p* | OR | 95% CI |
|-----------|------|------|------|------|------|--------|
| (Constant) | -4.21 | 1.08 | 15.19 | < .001 | | |
| Age (years) | 0.06 | 0.02 | 9.00 | .003 | 1.06 | [1.02, 1.10] |
| Gender (female) | 0.89 | 0.34 | 6.85 | .009 | 2.44 | [1.25, 4.75] |
| Income (per $10k) | -0.15 | 0.08 | 3.52 | .061 | 0.86 | [0.74, 1.01] |
| Education (college+) | 0.72 | 0.31 | 5.39 | .020 | 2.05 | [1.12, 3.77] |
| Smoking status | 1.23 | 0.38 | 10.48 | .001 | 3.42 | [1.62, 7.21] |

**Narrative for the table:**

> Table 2 presents the logistic regression coefficients. Smoking status was the strongest predictor of cardiovascular events, OR = 3.42, 95% CI [1.62, 7.21], *p* = .001, indicating that smokers had 3.42 times the odds of experiencing a cardiovascular event compared to non-smokers. Female gender, OR = 2.44, 95% CI [1.25, 4.75], and college education, OR = 2.05, 95% CI [1.12, 3.77], were also significant predictors. Income was not statistically significant at the .05 level, OR = 0.86, 95% CI [0.74, 1.01], *p* = .061.

### Hierarchical (Block) Entry

In hierarchical logistic regression, predictors are entered in sequential blocks to test whether adding a new set of variables significantly improves model fit beyond the previous block.

**APA reporting format for hierarchical models:**

> In Step 1, demographic variables (age and gender) were entered. The model was statistically significant, *chi-square*(2) = 14.32, *p* < .001, Nagelkerke *R*² = .12. In Step 2, behavioral variables (smoking and exercise) were added. The change in model fit was significant, *chi-square*(2) = 18.65, *p* < .001, and Nagelkerke *R*² increased from .12 to .27. The addition of behavioral variables explained an additional 15% of the variance in the outcome.

### Handling Categorical Predictors With Multiple Levels

When a categorical predictor has more than two levels (e.g., education: high school, college, graduate), it is represented by *k* - 1 dummy variables. Report the overall Wald chi-square for the variable as a set, followed by the individual comparisons.

> Education level was a significant predictor of treatment adherence, Wald *chi-square*(2) = 11.84, *p* = .003. Relative to high school education (reference), participants with college education had 2.15 times the odds of adherence, OR = 2.15, 95% CI [1.18, 3.92], *p* = .013, and those with graduate education had 3.40 times the odds, OR = 3.40, 95% CI [1.55, 7.45], *p* = .002.

## Assumptions of Logistic Regression

Logistic regression does not share the assumptions of linear regression (normality, homoscedasticity, linear relationship between predictors and outcome). However, it has its own set of assumptions that must be verified and reported.

### Assumption 1: Linearity of the Logit

Logistic regression assumes a linear relationship between each continuous predictor and the **log-odds** (logit) of the outcome. This does not mean the relationship between the predictor and the outcome probability is linear; rather, the relationship between the predictor and the log-transformed odds must be linear.

**How to test:** The standard approach is the Box-Tidwell test, which adds interaction terms between each continuous predictor and its natural logarithm to the model. A significant interaction suggests a violation of linearity.

**APA reporting format:**

> The linearity of the continuous variables with respect to the logit of the dependent variable was assessed via the Box-Tidwell procedure. All continuous independent variables were found to be linearly related to the logit of the dependent variable (all *p*s > .05).

If a violation is detected, consider transforming the variable (e.g., log transformation, polynomial terms) or categorizing it into meaningful groups.

### Assumption 2: Absence of Multicollinearity

When two or more predictors are highly correlated, the model becomes unstable: standard errors inflate, coefficients become unreliable, and individual predictor significance tests lose power.

**How to test:** Run a linear regression with the same predictors and inspect the **Variance Inflation Factor** (VIF) for each predictor.

| VIF Value | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------------|
| 1.0 | No collinearity |
| 1.0 - 5.0 | Moderate; generally acceptable |
| 5.0 - 10.0 | High; warrants investigation |
| > 10.0 | Severe; remedial action needed |

**APA reporting format:**

> Multicollinearity was assessed using variance inflation factors (VIF). All VIF values were below 2.5 (range: 1.08 - 2.34), indicating no problematic multicollinearity among the predictors.

**Remedies for multicollinearity:** Remove one of the correlated predictors, combine them into a composite variable, or use principal component regression.

### Assumption 3: Independence of Observations

Each case in the dataset must be independent of every other case. Violations occur with repeated measures, clustered data (e.g., students nested within classrooms), or matched designs.

If your data has a hierarchical structure, standard logistic regression is inappropriate. Use **multilevel logistic regression** (mixed-effects logistic regression) or **generalized estimating equations** (GEE) instead.

### Assumption 4: Adequate Sample Size

Logistic regression requires sufficient cases in both outcome categories for stable coefficient estimation. The classic rule of thumb is a minimum of 10 events per predictor variable (EPV), where "events" refers to cases in the less frequent outcome category.

| Number of Predictors | Minimum Events Needed (EPV = 10) | If minority class = 30% of N |
|---------------------|--------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 3 | 30 | N = 100 |
| 5 | 50 | N = 167 |
| 8 | 80 | N = 267 |
| 10 | 100 | N = 334 |

More recent simulation research suggests that **EPV = 20** provides more reliable estimates, and some authors recommend even higher thresholds for models with categorical predictors with many levels.

**APA reporting format:**

> The sample included 312 participants, of whom 94 (30.1%) experienced the outcome event. With five predictors in the model, the events-per-variable ratio was 18.8, exceeding the recommended minimum of 10.

### Assumption 5: No Influential Outliers

Outliers in logistic regression are cases that the model predicts very poorly. These are identified through diagnostic statistics such as **Cook's distance**, **leverage values**, and **standardized residuals**.

Cases with standardized residuals exceeding 2.5 (or 3.0 in some references) or Cook's distance greater than 1.0 should be examined individually. However, removal of outliers should be based on substantive reasons, not solely on statistical criteria.

## Common Mistakes in Logistic Regression Reporting

Even experienced researchers make preventable errors when reporting logistic regression results. The following mistakes appear frequently in submitted manuscripts and are among the most common reasons for revision requests.

### Mistake 1: Interpreting B Coefficients as Linear Effects

The *B* coefficient in logistic regression represents the change in **log-odds** per one-unit increase in the predictor. Unlike linear regression, where *B* represents a constant change in the outcome, the effect of a one-unit change in the predictor on the probability of the outcome varies depending on the baseline probability.

**Incorrect:** "For each additional year of age, the probability of disease increased by 0.06."

**Correct:** "For each additional year of age, the log-odds of disease increased by 0.06, corresponding to an odds ratio of 1.06."

The odds ratio is the exponentiated *B* coefficient (OR = e^*B*) and is the appropriate effect size to report and interpret.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Model Fit Statistics

Jumping directly to individual predictor results without first establishing that the overall model is significant is a structural error. The omnibus test, pseudo R-squared, and classification accuracy establish the foundation. If the overall model is not significant, individual predictor tests should be interpreted with great caution.

**A complete model fit section includes:**
1. Omnibus chi-square test with degrees of freedom and *p* value
2. Nagelkerke *R*² (and optionally Cox & Snell *R*²)
3. Hosmer-Lemeshow test
4. Classification accuracy with sensitivity and specificity
5. Comparison to the base rate

### Mistake 3: Missing Confidence Intervals for Odds Ratios

An odds ratio without a confidence interval is like a mean without a standard deviation. The point estimate tells you the most likely value, but the CI reveals how much uncertainty surrounds that estimate. Reviewers will almost always request CIs if they are missing.

### Mistake 4: Reporting Odds Ratios as Probability Ratios

This remains the most common interpretive error. The statement "patients who exercised were 2.5 times more likely to recover" implies a probability ratio, which is what a relative risk (RR) measures. The correct statement is "patients who exercised had 2.5 times the odds of recovery." When the outcome is common (prevalence > 10%), odds ratios substantially overestimate relative risks.

### Mistake 5: Not Specifying the Reference Category

For categorical predictors, the odds ratio compares a coded group to the reference category. Failing to specify which group serves as the reference makes the results uninterpretable. Always state the reference explicitly:

> Compared to participants with high school education (reference), those with college education had 2.15 times the odds of adherence.

### Mistake 6: Reporting Percentage Change Incorrectly

When converting an OR to a percentage change, use (OR - 1) x 100 for increased odds and (1 - OR) x 100 for decreased odds.

**Incorrect:** "OR = 1.35 represents a 35% increase" is sometimes stated without showing the calculation, leading readers to assume the percentage refers to probability rather than odds.

**Correct:** "For each additional unit of X, the odds of the outcome increased by 35% (OR = 1.35)." Be explicit that the percentage change refers to odds, not probability.

### Mistake 7: Omitting Assumption Checks

Not mentioning assumption verification (linearity of logit, multicollinearity, sample size adequacy) is a growing concern among reviewers. Even a brief statement such as "assumptions of logistic regression were examined and met" is better than silence, though providing specific test results is preferred.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between binary, multinomial, and ordinal logistic regression?

Binary logistic regression predicts a dichotomous outcome (two categories). Multinomial logistic regression predicts an outcome with three or more unordered categories (e.g., preferred transportation: car, bus, bicycle). Ordinal logistic regression predicts an outcome with ordered categories (e.g., disease severity: mild, moderate, severe). Each type produces odds ratios, but the interpretation and model structure differ. Binary is the most common and forms the foundation for the other variants.

### How do I interpret an odds ratio less than 1?

An OR less than 1.00 indicates that the predictor is associated with decreased odds of the outcome. To express the decrease as a percentage, calculate (1 - OR) x 100. For example, OR = 0.65 means that a one-unit increase in the predictor is associated with a 35% decrease in the odds of the outcome (1 - 0.65 = 0.35, or 35%). Some researchers take the reciprocal (1/0.65 = 1.54) and report it as the protective factor having 1.54 times the odds compared to the risk factor.

### What is the minimum sample size for logistic regression?

The classic guideline is at least 10 events per predictor variable (EPV), where events are cases in the less frequent outcome category. For a model with five predictors and a 30% event rate, you would need at least 50 events, requiring a total sample of approximately 167 participants. More recent research recommends EPV of 20 or higher for stable coefficient estimates, especially when predictors include multi-category variables.

### Should I use forward, backward, or enter method?

The **Enter** (forced entry) method is generally recommended for confirmatory research because it tests the specific set of predictors dictated by theory. **Forward** and **backward** stepwise methods are exploratory and capitalize on sample-specific characteristics, leading to models that may not replicate. If you use stepwise methods, report them as exploratory and validate the final model on an independent sample.

### How do I report logistic regression with interaction terms?

Report the main effects first, then the interaction term. An interaction in logistic regression means the effect of one predictor on the outcome depends on the level of another predictor. Report the interaction OR and explain what it means substantively. For example: "The interaction between treatment and gender was significant, OR = 2.18, 95% CI [1.12, 4.24], *p* = .022, indicating that the treatment effect on recovery was stronger for female participants than for male participants."

### Can I use R-squared to compare logistic regression models?

Pseudo R-squared values (such as Nagelkerke *R*²) can be compared across nested models (where one model is a subset of the other) fitted to the same dataset. However, they should not be compared across different datasets or different outcome variables because the values depend on the base rate and sample characteristics. For model comparison, use the likelihood ratio test, AIC, or BIC instead.

### What is the Wald test and how does it differ from the likelihood ratio test?

The Wald test evaluates whether each individual predictor's coefficient is significantly different from zero. It uses the ratio of the coefficient to its standard error. The likelihood ratio test compares the fit of two nested models (with and without the predictor). The likelihood ratio test is generally more reliable, especially for large coefficients where the Wald test can be conservative (the Hauck-Donner effect). For overall model evaluation, the omnibus likelihood ratio chi-square is preferred.

### How do I handle missing data in logistic regression?

Logistic regression with standard estimation (listwise deletion) drops all cases with any missing value on any variable, which can substantially reduce sample size and introduce bias. Consider multiple imputation if missingness exceeds 5% on any variable. Always report the amount of missing data and the method used to handle it. For example: "Of the original 450 participants, 412 (91.6%) had complete data on all variables and were included in the analysis."

## Logistic Regression APA Checklist

Before submitting your manuscript, verify that your logistic regression results include:

- The type of logistic regression performed (binary, multinomial, ordinal)
- Sample size and outcome group frequencies
- Overall model chi-square with degrees of freedom and *p* value
- Pseudo R-squared (Nagelkerke *R*² preferred) clearly labeled
- Classification accuracy (overall percentage, sensitivity, specificity)
- A coefficients table with *B*, *SE*, Wald *chi-square*, *p*, OR, and 95% CI for OR
- The intercept (constant) row in the coefficients table
- Narrative interpretation of odds ratios for significant predictors
- Odds ratios interpreted correctly (not as probabilities)
- All statistical symbols in italics (*B*, *SE*, *p*, *chi-square*, *R*²)
- Exact *p* values (or *p* < .001 for very small values)
- Assumption checks mentioned (linearity of logit, multicollinearity, outliers)
